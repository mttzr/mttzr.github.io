The Art of the Intro

November 13, 2024

A friend of mine, we’ll call Kyle, is looking to transition from working as a bench scientist to a business development (BD) role in biotech or pharma. When he mentioned this I offered to introduce him to a friend and former classmate of mine who happens to head BD at a major biotech company. We’ll call her Mary. Kyle was thrilled at the opportunity, but before making the introduction, I gave him an assignment: check the job boards at Mary’s company and see if there were any open roles on her team. This would lead to three possible introduction scenarios:

1. General Advice: I introduce Kyle to Mary and ask her to share advice on getting into BD.
2. Role-Specific Intro: I introduce Kyle to Mary as a candidate for a specific role on her team, highlighting why he’d be a great fit.
3. Proactive Pitch: I introduce Kyle to Mary with the suggestion that he’d be a fantastic addition to her team, even if no role currently exists, and encourage her to keep him in mind for the future.

The differences between these scenarios are substantial. In Scenario 1, Mary is doing me a favor by giving her time to share insights with Kyle—a gesture rooted in goodwill and little else. But in Scenarios 2 and 3, the dynamic shifts: Kyle becomes a potential asset to Mary, a “rockstar new hire” she may not have known she needed.

If Kyle isn’t interested in Mary’s company, then Scenario 1 is perfectly fine. However, if he’s even remotely interested in joining her team, it’s worth crafting a more thoughtful introduction along the lines of Scenario 2 or 3. First impressions matter. If we’re setting the stage for Kyle as an ideal candidate, we need to tell a compelling story that sparks excitement—especially in Scenario 3, where no job posting exists.

The Scenario 3 approach reminds me of an excellent blog post by a friend, Nan Ransohoff, about creating your own job opportunities rather than waiting for one to appear. It’s an underrated strategy. Many of the best candidates I’ve interviewed as a hiring manager were proactive in this way, showcasing initiative and creativity and creating FOMO in me.

Reflecting on this conversation, I was reminded that making introductions is an art. Early in my career, I was oblivious to this. I would jump into meetings or introductions with no clear goal, often realizing what I needed from the other person only after the conversation was over.

I learned a better approach from Deena Shakir, an incredible BD dealmaker I worked with during her time at Google [X]. Deena’s background—working for the Secretary of State, USAID, and as a BBC reporter—sharpened her negotiation and social acumen. Now a successful venture capitalist, she continues to exemplify the strategic thinking required to navigate high-stakes interactions.

Deena taught me the importance of doing my homework before requesting an introduction. What’s my goal? What value can I offer the other person? When it comes to introductions, it’s not just about making connections; it’s about setting the stage for success. The art of the intro lies in crafting an opportunity where both sides stand to gain something meaningful, after all everyone loves a win-win.
