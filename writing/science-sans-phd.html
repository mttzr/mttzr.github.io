Doing Science Without a PhD

June 20, 2024

My early twenties were marked by two key milestones: graduating from college and starting—and then stopping—graduate school. First, I tried medical school, only to realize I didn’t actually want to be a doctor. Then I gave a PhD program a shot, but I found that path wasn’t right for me either. I loved science and wanted to work in a technical field (this is why I kept applying to grad school), but the opportunity costs—time and money, but mostly time—felt too steep for both.

As a grad student, I often found myself distracted, spending hours scrolling through Hacker News and TechCrunch, envying the people out in the world building and launching products and companies. It seemed like I had another decade of studying ahead of me before I could achieve my true aspiration: build something impactful that changed lives. That envy eventually gave me the courage to leave school and move to San Francisco.

Now, when people ask about my academic background (this happened today at lunch with a scientist from a neighboring lab), I often joke that I’m “not a doctor two ways.”

But here’s the truth: doing science doesn’t require a PhD. While I greatly respect the rigorous training that comes with a PhD—so much so that I actively seek out PhDs (or those with similar advanced scientific degrees) to join my team—it’s neither a necessary nor sufficient condition for building scientific companies, organizations, or products.

Plenty has been written about the evolution (and sometimes, the critique) of higher education. I’m not here to add to that discourse. Instead, I want to share what my experience has been like working in biotech without a PhD, focusing on where it’s helped me and where it’s been a challenge.

Where It’s Helped

1. Asking Questions
Not having a PhD has freed me from the pressure of feeling like I should already know everything. I don’t hesitate to ask questions, which I believe is a superpower for any good leader. Curiosity is essential to innovation, and being comfortable with “not knowing” has opened doors to deeper understanding and collaboration.
2. Early Industry Experience
I spent my 20s bouncing between startups, learning how to build teams, identify product-market fit, and fundraise. These skills—what I think of as the “holy trinity” of company building—are hard to master from a textbook or academic environment. They require practice, and I’ve simply had more cycles to refine them.
3. No Attachment to Expertise
My time in biotech startups has been delightfully varied. I’ve worked on oncology diagnostics, drug discovery AI platforms, and RNA vaccines. This variety has brought me incredible joy because I’m constantly learning. I've seen more technical folks feel a strong attachment to their specific area of study—or even a fear branching out beyond it. I have no such attachment. Instead, I let my enthusiasm and excitement for a product or space guide my focus.

Where It’s Hurt

1. Fundraising with Traditional Biotech Investors
Some investors in biotech still hold traditional views about credentials. While I’ve successfully raised funding, I’ve sometimes had to work harder to build credibility without the “Dr.” in front of my name.
2. Imposter Syndrome in Highly Credentialed Spaces
Being surrounded by people with advanced degrees—particularly in highly technical or scientific discussions—can sometimes spark imposter syndrome. Even when I know I bring valuable skills to the table, it’s hard not to compare myself to others with more formal training.
3. Higher Barrier to Entry in Certain Technical Conversations
Not having spent years immersed in a specific scientific discipline during a PhD means I sometimes have to put in extra effort to prepare for highly technical conversations. While this has pushed me to become a stronger generalist, it can occasionally feel like a disadvantage in specialized discussions.

Should You Get a PhD?

If you’re on the fence about pursuing a PhD, the answer depends on your goals. Here’s my take:
• If you want to be a CSO or scientific leader: A PhD is often essential. It equips you with the technical depth and credibility needed to lead research programs and be recognized as an authority in your field.
• If you want to found a biotech company as a CEO: You don’t necessarily need a PhD, but you’ll need to build a strong team. This usually means finding a co-founder or key hire with deep scientific expertise to complement your skill set.

Final Thoughts

While I’ve occasionally felt the limitations of not having a PhD, I’ve mostly felt great pride in building my career outside the traditional academic path. Science thrives on diversity of thought, and the field needs leaders with a range of experiences—not just those who follow the well-trodden route.

Doing science without a PhD is entirely possible. What matters most is a clear sense of purpose, a willingness to learn, and the determination to build something meaningful.
