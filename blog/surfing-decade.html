Lessons From My First Decade of Surfing

June 13, 2024

Eleven years ago, I moved to SF and within my first seven days, I found myself at the beach, surfboard in hand, preparing to paddle out alongside a woman I met at a bar the night before. Neither of us knew it at the time, but we were about to become lifelong friends. The story of my time in SF is intertwined with my growth as a surfer. This coming weekend, I’m participating in my first surfing exhibition, an event specifically for queer surfers. Imagine a surf competition but with more heels on the beach and everyone gets a medal. I’m excited for the weekend, but not because I think I’m going to do well. In fact, I’ll be shocked if I do. I tend to get performance anxiety when it comes to this particular water sport. When I’m in the lineup and see a zoom lens on the beach, I end up spending so many mental cycles trying to focus on looking cool that I forget how to paddle or balance, and end up looking quite the opposite. And yet, I’m still excited. My excitement mostly comes from the fact that I’ll be paddling out in a heat, shoulder to shoulder with friendly and supportive surfers. This is the joy of the queer surf community; folks aren’t jockeying for waves, they’re just excited to see one another in the ocean. On the eve of this personal milestone event, I’m reflecting on what surfing has taught me over the past decade and what I hope the relationship looks like in the decades to come.

Lesson 1 - Non-Attachment
Like any good Bay Area technobro, I have read the first few chapters and the jacket covers of a number of Buddhist and pop-Buddhist texts. The buzzwords I can most crisply recall all have to do with the concept of non-attachment: detachment from an outcome, present-moment awareness, emotional balance, and acceptance—all concepts that surfing will throw in your face whether you’re ready or not. When you find your way to the water, some days are just plain bad. The conditions might not be right; it’s too windy, too choppy, too flat—it sometimes seems like there are more ways for the water to be wrong than right. And other days, you’re just not quite right—your paddle is weak, your balance is off, you get cold so quickly you check your suit for holes. And on some days, it’s a mix of all—both you and the ocean are just not having it. On these days, I’ve learned to just take a deep breath, catch one last wave in (or, if I’m really at a loss, just paddle back to shore), pack up, and go home. This might sound like I’m giving up, and in some micro sense, I am. In these moments, I’m choosing to give up on this particular day because I know that there are more days to surf, more waves to be caught. Just this last weekend, I made my way to a break near the town of Bolinas (a favorite of mine), only to arrive late due to a work commitment and to find my surf friend had already headed home and the waves were quite gusty and messy. The vibes weren’t right, so after 60 minutes in the water, I packed up and started my journey back. Seventy-two hours and one last-minute text later, I found myself back in the water with another friend for a sunset session at another break, catching glorious waves in the warm sunlight for well over three hours. The ocean giveth and the ocean taketh away. The lesson for me here is that there are always more waves. This is true of missing a wave in a set or scrapping an entire session. We can’t mourn what we missed out on or what failed to manifest, but just keep focused on the waves to come.

Lesson 2 - Progress is Beside the Point
If I told Matt from 10 years ago that I was still surfing, he’d be thrilled. But if I showed him a clip of me surfing today, he’d be like, “WTF?” (Matt from 10 years ago was very judgmental). The reason is that improvement in surfing is linear with time, but the slope of that improvement is shallow.


To be more precise, the day-by-day data points tend to be fairly scattered (at least in my chaotic case), but if you were to fit a line, that line would overall be positive… just a very mild gradient. But what proto-Matt circa 2013 wouldn’t be able to understand is that the graph doesn’t actually matter. The reason why he/I/we continue to surf isn’t to make progress on some graph (though, to be clear, the highs of making progress are fantastic), it’s just for the joy of doing the thing at all. The only way to enjoy surfing is to find joy in the good days and the bad days—the days when you make yourself so proud for catching a beautiful wave and gliding into the pocket, and the days when you’re just happy you didn’t drown. My love for the sport is consistent, and that’s the real achievement.

Lesson 3 - Everything is Better with Friends
I’m a social surfer, which is weird. For most people, surfing is a solo activity—a time to be alone with their thoughts and the ocean. I, on the other hand, love nothing more than paddling out with 1 to 50 friends, chatting constantly as we battle our way through the whitewash out to the calmness of the lineup. Once there, and once we’ve caught our breath, I can gab for hours (or whatever the period is between sets) about life at the day, week, month, or lifetime zoom level. One surf friend calls these discussions “board meetings” because they can carry some weight and big decisions can be made. Then, after we’ve made a catch (or attempted and fumbled), we cheer each other on from the lineup, celebrating our comrades as they make their way back out past the whitewater. For many years, I felt like I wasn’t a real surfer because I didn’t enjoy going by myself. When surfing alone, I find myself less motivated to stay in the water when it gets cold. I experience a lower dopamine rush when I catch a beautiful wave that I can’t share with a friend, and I feel more nervous when I wipe out hard and don’t have a friend to check that I’m still alive. However, a decade in, I’ve accepted that my preference for social surfing doesn’t make me any less of a surfer. In fact, the friendships I’m making are enhancing my experience. Away from cell service and other distractions, I find I’m able to be exceptionally present, splitting my attention only between the conversation and the waves forming out at sea. The folks I’ve connected with span many more demographics than I’d normally find in my cohort of obvious friends—but we are all united by our joy for the sport. One day, my therapist might fix me so I can stand spending large chunks of time alone with my thoughts. Until that day comes, I’m going to enjoy the friends I’m making along the way.

Lesson 4 - Spend More Time Giving yYour Body What It Wants
I did not grow up watching surfing or idolizing surf culture. In fact, by the time I moved from California to the East Coast for college, I had started to purposefully distance myself (at least mentally) from the surf and skate aesthetic that defined early 2000s teenage wardrobe—mine included. It was a culture that I participated in as a Californian teen, not one that I chose. But while I didn’t grow up with a love of surfing, or of athletics more generally, I have always enjoyed physical activity. I was never strong or coordinated, so ball sports and contact sports were off the table, but I did (and still do) have a lot of endurance. Living in a landlocked place, this endurance was put to use running outside. I ran track and I ran with my mom (it was basically a glass closet), and though I find the act of running boring, the slowly changing scenery of our suburban town provided enough of a distraction to get the job done. I tried a few times to run on a treadmill and nearly fell off just to mix things up. Lap swimming was far too monotonous, and while road biking can have beautiful changing scenery, I found the position on the bike feels unnatural—a strange subset of muscles activated while others are left totally inert. The amazing thing about surfing is that it is an endurance activity that engages basically every muscle but is so dynamic that no two waves (or two minutes) are exactly the same. Why do I love endurance sports? Because of how they make me feel afterward. Food tastes better after a derived hunger. I sleep better, and I just feel a high that lasts for hours. If each wave is a dopamine hit, the collective build-up of dopamine is even more powerful.

In closing, I hope that this still marks the beginning of my relationship with surfing. I aim to keep these four lessons top of mind while also remaining open to whatever else the sport and the ocean has to teach me.
